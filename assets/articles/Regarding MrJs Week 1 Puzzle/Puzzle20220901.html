Regarding Mr. Jones' Week 1 Puzzle

This is one of many puzzles provided by my maths teacher Mr. Samuel Jones. The goal here isn't to present a solution (since they are really not that hard...), but to study the puzzle itself and patterns emerges on the solutions as we iteratively changes the structure of this puzzle.

The puzzle asks to find the area enclosed by the rectangle that are excluded by the circles. Given that the diameter of the larger circle is 4.

Solution Scheme

With some geometry, one can obtain (usually through pythagorean theorem and circle properties) that the height of the rectangle is:

H = 3 + 8 = 3 + 2 2

The rests it takes are to calculate the areas and one step of subtraction.

The area of rectangle in this question will be:

A s = 12 + 8 2

The key of a problem is never so much the solution.

What can we do to the question?

A slightly different question

What if instead of having 2 small circles, we have 1 small circle with its area equals to the sum of areas of the 2 small circles?

π r 2 = π 1 2 + π 1 2 ⟹ r = 2

This requires 1 small circle with radias of 2^0.5. Such change can give out 2 possible configurations:

Configuration 1

Configuration 1 is self symmetric. Having a height of 4+2^0.5

H = 4 + 2

This leads to area of the rectangle to be:

A s = 16 + 4 2

Whereas the sum of areas of the circles remains to be:

A c = A r + A R = 6 π

Insetead of looking for the empty areas, we could compute the density of space filling circles by deviding its area with area of the rectangle:

ρ = A c / A s = 6 π 16 + 4 2 ≈ 0.87037

Configuration 2

The onlything that gets changed here is the height of rectangle (hence its area too).

One can obtain the distance between the two origins as shown above and computed below:

h = ( 2 + 2 ) 2 − ( 2 − 2 ) 2 = 2 2 2
H = ( 2 + 2 ) 2 − ( 2 − 2 ) 2 + ( 2 + 2 ) = 2 2 2 + 2 + 2

Leading to a density:

ρ = 6 π 8 + 4 2 + 8 2 2 ≈ 0.81351

The density of the original problem is: 0.80852.
